## Goodz onboarding answers from Mike

- Mike answered the saved pickup question about historical D2C winners.
- Historical D2C best-seller: **Mixtape Goodz**.
- Customers would buy a crate of about 5 or 6 Goodz that arrived blank, then assign playlists on first tap.
- This worked especially well as a gifting product, particularly for boyfriend/girlfriend / romantic gifting use cases.
- Strongest seasonal windows remembered: **Valentine's Day** and **Christmas**.
- Licensed / official-album Goodz did not gain nearly as much traction on the D2C side.
- Mike remembers two ad-creative types working especially well historically:
  - influencer-made ~30 second videos used as ads
  - explainer videos
- Once Janet reconnects more deeply with the Meta Ads project/backend, she should study which creative angles and campaigns performed best.
- Mike then clarified that the blank-on-arrival / assign-on-first-tap Mixtape flow was not a major problem in practice; support docs and package instructions made it work well.
- This shifts the likely D2C opportunity from fixing product confusion to improving landing-page / checkout conversion.
- Mike specifically endorsed an optional Spotify OAuth flow where customers could pull in favorite playlists/albums during purchase, choose what should arrive on the Goodz, and hand that off to manufacturing.
- Mike says manufacturing can support that preloaded-content flow without issue.
- Mike then answered the question about who Mixtape buyers were and what the deeper product opportunity might be.
- Primary Mixtape use cases were likely gifting to a romantic partner, family member, or friend, with a smaller but important self-purchase collector cohort.
- The self-purchase cohort is strategically interesting because they want a physical sense of ownership for streaming music.
- Mike believes the ownership experience has historically been weaker than the purchase intent: tap currently leads to a landing page and then another click out to Spotify/Apple Music, which feels cludgy.
- This points to a larger product opportunity around owned-Goodz UX, not just checkout UX.
- Mike is interested in concepts such as richer owned pages, embedded playlist/media context, art/notes personalization, visual collection pages, browsing others' collections, and potentially a broader "Discogs for the streaming generation" vision.
- Younger users who never built physical music collections may be a particularly strong target if Goodz becomes a status/style/personality layer as well as a gift object.
- Mike then answered the B2B repeatability question in a very useful way.
- Major record labels have been among the least scalable B2B categories due to rights/category issues: Goodz behaves more like merch than CD/vinyl, which creates structural friction.
- Major-label work that did happen usually came from marketing budgets for giveaways / superfan events, which are sporadic, last-minute, and unreliable.
- Direct consumer licensing deals for official artwork are possible but expensive and too narrow to support a broad catalog strategy.
- Promising / more repeatable B2B-ish paths according to Mike:
  - weddings (if planner/channel access improves)
  - luxury real estate (already has some repeat-buyer evidence)
  - trade shows / unique leave-behinds
  - smaller labels and indie artists
- Bandcamp / indie artist merch is a particularly attractive concept: Goodz could be sold in bulk, then resold by bands on Bandcamp or on tour, potentially with Bandcamp download codes loaded onto the Goodz.
- Mike sees that as environmentally friendlier, cheaper, and more functional than vinyl for many fans.
- Mike then answered the near-term B2B prioritization question.
- Practical answer: focus first on indie artists.
- Timing logic matters: with April 1 arriving and a few weeks likely needed for build + ad setup, weddings may be a weaker first push because much of the immediate wedding season is likely already committed.
- Better sequencing may be indie artists first, then weddings / other B2B verticals in summer to target fall/winter events.
- Mike then refined the indie-artist wedge further: DJs are attractive because they can sell mixes; touring indie bands and strong-Bandcamp artists also seem promising; small labels are appealing for repeatability/scale but may be harder to reach.
- A key next-step question is practical distribution/targeting: which of these audiences can Goodz actually reach in a cost-effective and targetable way through ads or other channels.
